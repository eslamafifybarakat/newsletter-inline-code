Leadership Newsletter – Promotion & Psychological Readiness
Promotion Doesn’t Mean Psychological Readiness
Hello,

A promotion is often seen as a success. A higher position. More trust. Broader responsibility.

But many leaders discover months later that pressure increased… comfort decreased… and an internal question starts to form: “I’m excellent at my work… why don’t I feel at ease?”

Lara Hogan, a leadership coach and author, wrote honestly about the hardest part of management. For her, it wasn’t a lack of technical skill, but the shock of transitioning from an execution role to one that depends on people, context, and waiting.

In her writings, especially about the levels of management, she explains that what wears down new leaders isn’t incompetence — it’s that the nature of success itself changes: Before: you deliver with your own hands.
After: you succeed when others succeed, often with delayed results.

And that’s where confusion sets in.
⚖️
What Changes After a Promotion
A promotion doesn’t just change your tasks — it changes the source of your psychological satisfaction.

Before: I accomplished, I finished, I did it myself.
After: I guided, I waited, I helped others achieve.

This transition is exhausting because you often:
  • Lose direct control
  • Lose the clarity of quick wins
  • Enter a new role without enough psychological tools
📊
Why This Transition Is So Draining
Many leaders misinterpret this tension as “I’m not fit for the role.”
But the truth is often: this is a natural shift between two different professional identities.

Lara Hogan describes it as the hardest part of management: moving from doing to enabling, from certainty to ambiguity, from immediate results to delayed outcomes.

The problem isn’t the person — it’s the lack of psychological scaffolding for the new role.
🛡️
Steps to Ease the Psychological Shift
1. Temporarily change your success metrics.
Are you still measuring yourself by the standards of your old role?

2. Ask for guidance, not just evaluation.
“What leadership skill do I most need to learn right now?”

3. Allow yourself to be a beginner.
Technical expertise does not mean immediate psychological readiness.
A promotion is not the end of struggle — it’s the beginning of a new kind.

The conscious leader doesn’t self‑flagellate over this transition — they learn through it.
